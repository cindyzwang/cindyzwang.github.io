Intro

When I got to college, I had no idea what I wanted to do. I had some AP credits that gave me the freedom to take whatever I wanted for a bit and I took some really interesting classes: neuroscience, linguistics, globalization and poverty, fencing...

But I was sick of doing work that didn't build up to anything, so I knew I had to just pick a path. I wanted to do something in STEM and I wasn't picky so I googled Boston University. The Wikipedia page at the time said it had the 8th best Biomedical Engineering program in the country, so I went with it.

"Science is about knowing; engineering is about doing" - Henry Petroski

At the end of those 3 years taking science and engeering courses, I knew I couldn't practice science—it's a lot of liquid handling. But advancing our understanding of biology and physiology is still something that I get really excited about and problems we face in these areas are problems that I want to work on, so my career goal is to engineer solutions to interesting problems within biology and medicine.

About

You can view my resume  here.

I grew up in St. Louis, went to Boston University, got a B.S. in Biomedical Engineering, and moved out to San Francisco to work in biotech.

How did I get into software, you ask?

When I declared biomedical engineering as my major, my AP credits and some of the random classes I took freshman year meant that I could pretty much just jump in at the same place as all of classmates and graduate on time ðŸ˜Ž. And my computer science coursework (Java) took the place of the engineering computation class (MATLAB) that turned out to be a very important prereq for several classes. So I taught myself MATLAB.

Then I got a research assistantship at a neuroscience lab at a Veterans Affairs hospital. The principle investigator wanted to observe brain activity to identify markers for long-term versus short-term learning. The goal was to use these markers to develop effective learning techniques for Alzheimer's patients. My job was to write scripts to analyze electroencephalography data. It had been years since I took Neuro 101 and I never took the MATLAB course so I didn't know if this would actually turn out well. It turns out, learning programming the CS way meant that I could make more dynamic MATLAB scripts. I wouldn't have to add more code as the datasets got bigger, or if I wanted to observe different brain regions, or if I wanted to look at a subset of the data. I just put these options into a user interface. I didn't realize how advantageous it was to have CS skills as a biomedical engineer until I put it into good use. Biotech and medtech give me a chance to help accelerate research and the development of new treatments and therapies. That is why I'm so passionate—plenty have said stubborn or picky—about using CS to work on interesting problems. I make a lot of jokes about how a google search put me in biotech but I couldn't be happier to work on tough and important problems.
